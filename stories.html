## 14 Days

**Day One** The angel comes to your door at midnight. “By the way,” it says, “can I live with you for fourteen days?” “Sure.” There’s no other answer. You step aside, letting in a swirl of muggy summer air and an angel. It wipes its feet on the mat. Snow. It’s come a long way—it’s early August here in the rural Northwest. “The only rule is that I must maintain total silence, except for one moment each night, in which I will ask if you wish to wake again tomorrow.” “Do you want anything to drink?” You say. “Water? Tea?” The angel makes a motion with four of its hands. “(Soup sounds good.)” You have a container of minestrone in your fridge from your mom’s last visit. The noodles are bloated from swimming in broth for so long. You were saving it for a bad day. Lately all the days have been bad. “(I noticed),” says the angel. It’s bad at being quiet, speaking in parentheticals to avoid notice. You put the soup in the microwave. Sixty seconds of soft whining. The angel stands on the other side of the room so the radiation doesn’t disturb its brain chemistry. The spoon clinks in the glass container. Splash of broth on your wrist. You wipe it off with a dish towel instead of licking your wrist, which feels too vulgar a gesture while under the eye of God. “I can dig the inflatable mattress out of storage tomorrow,” you say, watching the angel eat. It looks at you blankly. It takes another spoonful of soup, slurping. “Right. The silence rule.” It plays pretty fast-and-loose with that one. The angel finishes the soup, and speaks. “Do you wish to continue living?” **Day Two** You wake up at 7am. The angel is still sleeping, so you dress in the bathroom. Long sleeves, work pants; hat, gloves, and bandana in a bag. Breakfast is an omelette once you get the stove running. Three eggs, even though your stomach is dull with sleep. The angel comes downstairs and chops scallions unevenly for you. “Thanks.” The morning sun turns the angel’s body translucent. It’s been a while since you shared a bed with a stranger. “I have to go to work,” you say, “but you can... do whatever you want here, really.” The angel tilts its head. You’d rather it not come with you. It notices. You leave. Carpool with the other workers—Alice, your boss, drives. Her daughter, nine, plays with a fluffy keychain in the front seat. Alice speaks in rapid Mandarin with the other workers, except the two American ones. Students. The girl has a crush on the guy, who knows but doesn’t like her back. You arrive at the farm. You sign in, put your bag under one of the plastic tables in the shade of a tent, and get to work. Bandana, gloves, collect the garlic, clean it up, cast it away, again. You think about the angel as you move through the first row in the field. You answered yes last night, obviously. You shake a clump of dirt until the bulb in the center is visible; you brush through the hairy roots gently but efficiently, and toss the garlic into the nearest pile. Green, purple, white. One of the other workers, Ethan, joins your row, and you make easy conversation about his art degree and a new TV show. He flirts with you, a little. You match it, and he stops. By lunch, your skin is damp with sweat and gritty with dirt. When you take your gloves off to wash your hands, there are brown rings around your wrists. Your nails have had dirt under them all summer. Alice serves lunch, picked up from the city. Everyone squabbles over big vats of chicken and rice and trays of cold watermelon, the metal fogged up from the fridge. You eat voraciously, only noticing the cut after you’ve finished off the watermelon. You wipe your mouth with your hand and realize you’re bleeding. Red sluggishly smears across your knuckles. You’ve almost forgotten about the angel when you get home late in the afternoon. You shower the dirt off, cook, and eat on the couch with your feet on the coffee table, watching the news. You used to work on wood models in the evenings. Your one indulgence; expensive kits. The last one you started was a fox. You don’t have the energy anymore. It sits, ribcage-open, on your desk. Stepping forward, incomplete, reaching for something you haven’t built yet. “Do you like your job?” The angel asks. “Shit,” you startle, “where’d you come from?” “The other room.” Smartass. Except it’s an angel, so you don’t say that. You put your feet down, and turn the TV off. “What did you do all day?” “Do you like your job?” “I thought you weren’t supposed to talk.” You yawn. “Yeah, I love it.” The angel is stupefied. “(But you are so sad.)” You laugh. “I’m just tired. Eight hours of physical labour.” Do you look sad? You’re conscious of the fact that you’re eating alone. “I like it,” you say, feeling self-conscious. “The sun, the money. I like the work. It’s physical.” You feel fulfilled when you come home aching and sweaty. “It’s seasonal, though. I work in an office the rest of the year.” You shrug, and take a bite of food to end the conversation. You were hungry a moment ago, but now you have to work to chew and swallow. You don’t like talking about yourself. “(Oh.) Do you wish to wake again tomorrow?” “Yeah, man. Sorry.” You didn’t mean to call it man. It turns the TV on. **Days Three, Four, And Five* “Do you wish to wake again tomorrow?” Yes. “Do you wish to wake again tomorrow?” Uh huh. “Do you wish to wake again tomorrow?” Why not. *Day Six* You forget your bandana in the morning because of a conversation with the angel. You’re sneezing dirt all day. The conversation goes like this: “Your brother telephoned.” “Thanks. I forgot to call him back this weekend.” “He loves you.” “I know. He tell you that?” You try to picture the phone call. Ring, ring, hello, I’m the angel, I love my sister, thanks, I shall tell her. “No...” The angel spins its fiery wings. “He’s thinking of proposing to Andrea.” You flinch. “No, he’s not, he would’ve told me.” “He’s afraid that telling you will rub your loneliness in your face.” You let out a deep breath. Your mother called you Mellow as a baby. You never cried. You’re always happy. “I’m not alone, I have my family.” “I didn’t say alone, I said lonely.” “What’s wrong with you? Why are you doing this?” You never cried. You never screamed. The geyser bubbles. “YOU’RE NOT EVEN SUPPOSED. TO TALK.” And so you forget your bandana when you leave for work. You realize you’ve been strangling your emotions before you can feel them. You haven’t felt much for a while. Only glimpses of sunlight. Now your chest is hot with anger as you stack bulbs in a bin. The angel is wrong. The tractor roars toward you, bright red. You haul the bin onto the back with a grunt. The angel is lying. You finish your rows in record time. You blow the dirt out of your nose on the ride home, the sharp smell of garlic and earth never leaving. Standing outside the apartment, you call your brother. On the second try, he picks up. “Hey\!” “Am I invited to the wedding?” “What?” “The wedding. You and Andrea.” “There’s no—” He lets out a long breath. “Of course you’re invited. Who told you?” You think for a while. You lean against the brick wall of the apartment building. You are glad he didn’t lie. “You told my... my roommate. On the phone.” “Aw, c’mon, was it papa? I know you don’t have a roommate, and I haven’t talked to anyone.” Right. “They’re here on a trial basis. Nah, two weeks. Their name? Uh. We haven’t gotten that far yet. No, I don’t think so. Well. Maybe. I don’t... It's nice to have company. But they said something pretty hurtful today. It was about, ah, don’t worry about it. It was maybe too honest.” *Day Seven* You’re still angry. You relish it. Everything feels brighter under the passion of anger. You remember only a few other times, in childhood, wrestling in your grandmother’s apartment with your cousins, that you felt the world was so saturated. The lines on your palms continue forever, the sky burns itself into your eyes, the wetness of the earth is now enjoyable, lunch and dinner taste better, you want to work on the fox. You turn on the little desk lamp you bought, warm yellow, and get to work, gluing two pieces of wood together according to the instructions you flip through. You brace the wood so it’ll dry correctly. The angel sits on the ceiling, watching. It hasn't spoken today. Holier-than-thou. You mark a piece of wood every one and a half centimeters with more force than necessary. “How will the pieces fit together?” The wood snaps. You forgot its voice could be so loud, so jarring, so absolute. You give in to frustration—why do you have to ruin everything?—when something floats from above. The angel shed a feather, and it drifts down to the wood. Where it lands it dissolves into the grain, mending the splinter. You look up at the angel. “Thank you.” The anger bursts, but the clarity stays. The overwhelming vividness of the universe. Night. The angel, sprawled on the inflatable mattress, asks questions like it’s at a sleepover. “Do you feel like you have a purpose? I don’t like secrets. Is it ethical to exist when you only live to die? I was only trying to help you. Why are garlic scapes called scapes? Do you wish to wake again tomorrow?” “Goodnight, angel.” *Day Eight* The angel doesn’t pretend not to talk anymore. The days of parenthetical whispers are over. The angel comes to work with you. It cooks you breakfast, maybe still sorry, maybe just because it wants to. It has a fascination with your stove, and eating: it watches you eat, always. "I don’t usually put things in my body. It is disturbing... like performance art." You swallow your mouthful of coffee. "Cool." It rides on the roof of the car to follow you to work. Alice doesn't notice. The passersbys gape. You stop beside a trucker at a traffic light, and his cigarette drops from his mouth. You wave. The angel is big. You can see where the car ceiling buckles under its weight. You press a hand to it. Nobody in the car asks why. The angel turns into a worm when you arrive at the farm. You like worms fine, but you tell it you're not putting one in your pocket. "Would a beetle suffice?" "God, it's so disturbing to hear a worm talking. Ah, I didn’t mean—sorry. Forget I said that." "We don't have to talk about God," the angel says. Alice shouts, saving you from answering. “Yeah, alright\!” To the worm: “She wants me to do three more rows by lunch. No way. I’m being paid minimum wage.” The worm turns into a ladybug, and flutters to rest on your hat. “Mm m mromm bmms.” “You’re too tiny, can’t hear you.” The ladybug flutters away, then develops back into an angel. It perches on the empty tractor nearby. “You might do three rows if you stop insulting worms and start working.” Ethan, eleven rows over, screams. You turn back to the mud clods you’re stomping apart. “Yea? That’s a lot of backtalk from someone who’s harvested a total of zero garlic today.” The angel rolls six eyes and flies over. Rings spinning, it lofts green shoots out of the earth in a broad circle around you. The garlic hangs in the sky for a moment, the dangling wine bulbs still dripping dirt, before the angel tosses everything haphazardly at the tractor. You put your hands on your hips and look at the mess. “Huh.” You decide it’s time to clock out for the day. You sign out dutifully, the angel watching over your shoulder. “What do you wish to do now?” It asks. “Go to a Dolly Parton concert.” “We could play her music loudly in the living room.” “Deal.” It turns out angels can function as speakers. “I can see sound waves and modify them,” it explains. “I’m beggin’ of you, pleeaaasse don’t take my maaan,” you reply. The angel likes Jolene the best, and keeps looping it. That’s because the angel is basic. Its rings spin in dance, sparking out flame. You think it is happy. “You know,” you say, catching your breath from a particularly rousing round of Jolene, “when I was little, I wanted to be a fire eater.” The angel flares its fire. “Many young children are fascinated with controlling something dangerous.” “Yeah, and eating fire is cool as hell.” The angel doesn’t react to the mention of hell, so you continue. “My ma would’ve killed me, though. Although, when she caught me eating matches once, she just rolled her eyes and left the room.” “Your stupidity is stunning.” “Was, was stunning.” The angel creates a sphere of flame in the centre of one of its spinning rings. It burns cerulean blue with a licking white exterior. The angel throws the sphere upward, your eyes tracking it, then swallows. You see the flame travel through its body before snuffing out. The angel then uses a wing to swat the ceiling, which caught fire. You stare at the spot, soot flaking off and floating to rest on the angel’s wings. It shakes them clean. You aren’t getting your deposit back. “Do you wish to wake again tomorrow?” “Yes.” There’s a day after that, a day after that, stretching beautifully out—but you can only see a dull, stinking, dead day, after the fourteen beautiful ones. Six, now. You don’t want it to leave, you realize. The rules were for it to live with you. For it to be silent. For it to ask you The Question every night. It does nothing with your answers to The Question, only acknowledging your answers to other questions it asks. When it breaks the silence. The silence that has permeated your apartment, your body, your life, for years. *Day Nine* How do you make an angel stay? Did it come to you of free will? Will the same forces guide it away again? You take a big bite of the sandwich it helped you make (toasting the bread with its rings, wheeling around the slices like a holy toaster) as you ponder the question now batting at your consciousness. You’re on a park bench in the August heat, soaking up warmth from the wood slats as the sun strays behind a cloud. The air is stagnant, but pleasantly so; calm, hot. The angel sits next to you on the bench. You think it’s doing something, because nobody who walks by notices it. The angel predicts the weather for the coming days and tells you the caloric value of your sandwich. How do you make *anyone* stay? You don’t. You think it might. You hope it will. It seems pointless to go to work today. Alice doesn’t pay you much anyway. You do it because you want to, you realize. You like it. But the things you like about it can be found in other places, too, and it’s summer, and maybe you deserve one day off. Alice texts you angrily, but the farm will survive one day without you. *Day Ten* You work on the fox again today, but it unsettles you. The uncertainty. The closer you get to finishing it, the more the feeling lingers. Where is the fox going? You don’t have instructions for the rest of the scene. You only can build the fox. You don’t know what the world around it looks like. You finish the limbs—the front leg, the paw, still stepping forward, reaching for something. *Day Eleven* “What are you going to wear to the wedding?” It feels like such a distant prospect. A hypothetical. “I don’t know. My red dress. Maybe I’ll get a suit.” “What are you looking forward to about it?” You notice the angel doesn’t give you the option to not be excited. “Seeing my family.” You smile despite yourself. “My brother and his family, but also all my cousins, my grandparents, my aunts and uncles. One of my cousins just had a baby. I hope they bring her.” You saw pictures. Her rosy little cheeks, chubby hands. Everyone taking turns holding her—you want to do that, to coo over a swaddled gurgling bundle. “They will. You will look good in the suit.” “One could say it’ll *suit* me.” The angel hits you with a wing. “Oops.” “Not fair. What’re you wearing?” *Day Twelve* Maybe if you were better. Here, this life with the morning light and the meals cooked together, the tv with the melted corner because the angel got angry at your sitcom. Here, with the future so blank. So fast. Here, at stake. *Day Thirteen* You fight with the angel. About petty things. Because you’re scared. “I’m not *scared*, stop saying that. I’m not scared, I’m irritated. There’s feathers all over the floor, no matter how many times I tell you to stop shedding.” You save them in a drawer. “The wind will take them.” “There’s no wind\!” “Open the window.” You scream in frustration for one white-hot second, then walk over and open the window. The breeze heats your face, and you put your hand out to feel it, dizzy. You shut the window. “There’s one more day left,” you say, looking back. The angel meets your gaze, unflinching. You feel as you do when you stare into the eyes of an animal too long. There’s another being breathing, twitching, in there. Just as you look out from your own body. “Do you wish to continue living?” “Yes. Fuck.” *Day Fourteen* On the fourteenth day, you wake up late. The angel asks you the question while you lay in bed. You roll over and look at it: glorious, all-seeing, of many eyes. Bedhead. On your old mattress with a wool blanket you found from the previous owner. You tell it you don’t know. The angel says it wants to bring you someplace. The drive is long, and you fall asleep as the angel’s many capable hands hold the wheel. You trust it to take you where you are meant to be. The angel doesn’t wake you up. You wake up long after it has parked. “How long was I out?” The angel shrugs, and looks away. It gets out of the car, and you follow—it’s somber today, still and lambent. It took you to a shady spot, dark and quiet, leafy green all around you, palpitating. The angel leads you to a small hiking trail, and you follow its silence, winding about the woods. Its wings drape across the forest floor, gathering dirt. You want to hold them in safety but you don’t. You feel a bit shy in wake of its silence. The sound of water laps at you until you reach the creek. The angel says nothing, still, merely is there, has brought you here, saying look: isn't it so gorgeous you want to weep? This is life. The creek undulates through a small ravine, puddling on the rocky shore on occasion. Eroded dirt on the banks expose roots, the twisting lifeblood of the starry masses of trees encompassing you. The sun shines dimly on the rounded rocks in the creek, and blindingly on the water and the tallest leaves above you; the water is green and brown from the mossy stones it flows over, but where the sun alights, it transforms to brilliant white and glowing blue. Several tipped trees and crashed logs bridge the stream. The angel makes its way toward one, and you follow, stepping where it did, pressing your feet into the muddy prints it creates and then abandons. It climbs onto a log, and you clamber on as well, finding your footing only after you almost tip off. Instead of crossing the creek, it sits in the middle of the log, wingtips dripping into the water rushing below. You sit beside it though you're curious about the other side of the forest. You can cross later, can’t you? The angel ends its silence. “What is your name?” It still manages to surprise you. “Joan,” you say. “What’s yours?” You glance over at it, the half-turn old conversation partners make when they already know what the other looks like, what face they will be making; a glance merely to assure oneself of the other. And you just find the trees, the water, birdsong. The world on the other side of the creek. Waiting.

## The Primitive

Given that primitivism stems from othering everything outside of Western culture, it’s still present in contemporary culture. Performance art / “ragebait” like Kate Shumskaya’s Instagram series I’m tired of being normal exemplifies contemporary primitivism by mocking highbrow European dining norms through eating with her hands in upscale restaurants. By behaving this way to portray her exhaustion with inhibited European culture, she implies that eating with one’s hands (as is customary with specific dishes in Ethiopia, Southeast Asia, Nigeria, etc) isn’t “normal”. As Ratcliff defines it, primitivism is “an attempt, made within the confines of ‘high culture,’ to enjoy the supposed benefits of ‘low’ or alien culture.” Primitivism is a problematic tool of resistance. Shumskaya’s series, however, is a less overt example than the white-climate-activist obsession with Indigenous people. White people hold a simultaneous fascination and degradation of Indigenous people’s customs and appearances. Speaking locally, the Coast Salish are mysticized, assumed to have ancient wisdom and a magical connection to nature. This manifests in settlers fetishizing the Coast Salish, buying dreamcatchers, and getting tattoos in the traditional geometric art style they have no connection to outside of what they assume it symbolizes–a break from Western ecocide. This ultimately undermines the effort to respect Indigenous knowledge in conservation efforts. Like Gauguin, many Canadians obsess over the aesthetic of Indigenous people without putting in effort to know individuals. Cultural iconography becomes devoid of its original meaning, transforming into a symbol of opposition to oppressive Western beliefs, but ultimately, in its usage, perpetuating those values.
