## The Big Toe's Revolutionary Beauty

Surrealism is defined as "psychic automatism" by Breton in his 1924 manifesto. As co-founder of the movement, Breton was influenced by Freud into believing in dreams as weapons, that revolution would come from the subconscious absence of control and reason. He argued that movements like Dadaism which borrow propagandistic iconography in order to critique it were ineffective. Surrealism also recognized the absurdity of the world, but responded by unleashing unconscious desire and fear through the uncanny and the marvelous, with the goal of self discovery and revolution. Bataille also believed in destroying polite society, but thought Breton’s focus on common desires and beauty had no revolutionary future. In his art magazine Documents, Bataille wrote about the big toe and its representation of baseness. Accompanying this article were photographs by Boiffard, a Surrealist photographer interested in experimental lighting techniques. His photographs transform the ordinary foot through scale and lighting to create a disturbing symbol of revolution for interwar France. In his article, Bataille argues that feet are condemned by superficial men due to the impure, brutal seduction induced by the foot’s taboo. He insists on questioning seductiveness without “taking into account poetic concoctions” such as Breton’s sexualized women. Bataille calls for a return to reality, saying such an awakening implies “one is seduced in a base manner, without transpositions and to the point of screaming, opening his eyes wide … before a big toe.” In modern society, we consume unquestioningly. Physical and cyber spaces teem with diversionary advertisements seducing us into blind acceptance through docile beauty. To counter that, we need to embrace base beauty such as the deformities and fetishes that make us human. Bataille’s work is a warning. We need to look down to our feet, to reality, instead of losing ourselves in the constructed upper reality (built on the lower) that covers our eyes. Bataille says though we are the "most noble of animals," we nevertheless have feet, which "independently lead an ignoble life." Revolution comes from those unafraid to notice the foundations (feet) our societies are built on.

## The Primitive

Given that primitivism stems from othering everything outside of Western culture, it’s still present in contemporary culture. Performance art / “ragebait” like Kate Shumskaya’s Instagram series I’m tired of being normal exemplifies contemporary primitivism by mocking highbrow European dining norms through eating with her hands in upscale restaurants. By behaving this way to portray her exhaustion with inhibited European culture, she implies that eating with one’s hands (as is customary with specific dishes in Ethiopia, Southeast Asia, Nigeria, etc) isn’t “normal”. As Ratcliff defines it, primitivism is “an attempt, made within the confines of ‘high culture,’ to enjoy the supposed benefits of ‘low’ or alien culture.” Primitivism is a problematic tool of resistance. Shumskaya’s series, however, is a less overt example than the white-climate-activist obsession with Indigenous people. White people hold a simultaneous fascination and degradation of Indigenous people’s customs and appearances. Speaking locally, the Coast Salish are mysticized, assumed to have ancient wisdom and a magical connection to nature. This manifests in settlers fetishizing the Coast Salish, buying dreamcatchers, and getting tattoos in the traditional geometric art style they have no connection to outside of what they assume it symbolizes–a break from Western ecocide. This ultimately undermines the effort to respect Indigenous knowledge in conservation efforts. Like Gauguin, many Canadians obsess over the aesthetic of Indigenous people without putting in effort to know individuals. Cultural iconography becomes devoid of its original meaning, transforming into a symbol of opposition to oppressive Western beliefs, but ultimately, in its usage, perpetuating those values.

## Bodies Meeting the World: Edge Effects

As the Crane Flies by Jin-me Yoon considers the complex co-existence of North and South Korea through film of an old military ground near the DMZ. She uses a thermal camera, just as South Korean military surveillance and border security do. Placed inside a small room, a “bunker,” you are bombarded by sound... outside the room, the sound bleeds, relating to edge effects by mimicking how politics between borders interact. In addition to (as mentioned on her website) sonic meditation and warfare, Yoon focuses on the power of sound to propose new futures. The DMZ is incredibly biodiverse, a reminder that the borders created by war are permeable. Within earshot of Yoon’s bunker are three lively drawings. In Chambers’ selected pieces by Fawkes, thick black lines define nudes thrumming with color. Her drawings are held within white frames on a white wall, just as her drawn bodies are contained by their edges. Chambers speaks of “vectors of movement” and “bodily gestures across time and space” to describe her selections. Formal choices Fawkes made that reinforce those ideas are how her colors scribble in bound tangles of energy, and divide the body according to imagined light. From Muybridge to Duchamp, artists continually strive to represent the duration of experience; to collapse time and space into one image. If edge effects are when two communities meet, then the two communities meeting in Fawkes’ works are the body and the space it interacts with, or movement meeting space, or the body meeting art. Although mounted in a traditional museum format, the piece then forwards interaction: our body meets this body of art. Chambers/Fawkes and Yoon both use medium to capture life (color, thermal cameras). Crucially, they both think about the movement of a body traversing space. Yoon focuses on bodies crossing borders, while Chambers thinks more broadly about bodies through space. In terms of “edge effects”, Chambers considers the meeting of the body with the world. Yoon represents two countries interacting, as well as the past meeting the present and the future through sonic transformations.
